Welcome to CO2-GASP

    This software CO2-GASP enables a wide variety of users to model and interact with groundwater and geothermal data from the contiguous continental states. See the about page for a more detailed description of the methodology employed here.
You have the option of either modelling geothermal and free state CO2 data or geochemical modelling.

Option 1) - Geothermal and free state CO2 data

For selected areas and variations in subsurface-depth this modelling option enables a user to generate maps and graphs of;
  • CO2 density (fig. A)
  • CO2 state (e.g. gas, liquid or supercritical) (fig. B)
  • Temperature at formation depth (see here)
  • Rasters of underlying MODIS landsurface temperature and SMUH geothermal gradient data (see here)
For further details see information about the input datasets (geothermal gradient data and surface temperature data), the method and example results.

Option 2) - Geochemical modelling

For selected areas this geochemical modelling (PHREEQC-Pitzer) evaluates the interaction between injected CO2, the formation waters and the local mineralogy. The modelling drives each individual water to a temperature and pressure estimated to represent the conditions at the depth from which it was sampled (the at-formation depth).
    The user supplies a mineralogical composition that is thought to represent a formation of interest. Each water, now at-formation depth conditions, is initially equilibriated with the lithology to correct any discrepancy in the initial water chemistry. Thereafter in a second stage, CO2 is injected up to a pressure equal to the at-formation depth conditions. The data from this stage is the primary data that is reported and used in further calculations.
    Using the simulation data the user is given an option to calculate the volume, and for a given price per tonne ($70 per tonne) the value, of CO2 that could be stored in a hypothetical water-satured cylinder of a given porosity. This cylinder represents a volume of rock surrounding the injection well in which the water in pore spaces saturates with CO2 with thhe solubility of CO2 determined by the earlier modelling.
  • The calculated solubility of CO2 in the produced water phase. This data is then extrapolated to the theoretical water saturated cylinder (figs. C & D).
  • The change in mineralogy and other key PHREEQC simulation data for each simulation.
  • The change in porosity. Specifically the difference between the initial mineral volume and the final mineral volumes is calculated. CO2 injection typically lowers the pH driving dissolution and/or precipitation of different minerals.
For further details see information about the input datasets (produced water database, geothermal gradient data and surface temperature data), the method and example results.
