2.1.5 Robertson et al. (2021)

In this section for background reference we describe the key findings and, more pertinently, the groundtruthing of the coupled datasets from Robertson et al. (2021) (where further details can be found).
    The dissolution of dollsmite is described by the following equation;
CaMg(CO3)(2)(s) <--> Ca2+(aq)+ Mg2+(aq) + 2CO32-(aq) (eq.1)

    The solubility product constant (Ksp-dol) describes the amount of dolomite that needs to dissolve/precipitate for this equation to achieve equilibrium. For dolomite it can be expressed as such;
Ksp-dol = (aCa2+)(aMg2+)(aCO32)2 (eq.2)

    Note a represents the activities of ions in solution. Activities approximately reflect the concentration of an ion in solution and are calculated by PHREEQC (Pitzer model). The groundwater method of determining the K(sp-dol) assumes that the water is also in equilibrium with calcite (for which we have proven for >90% of US groundwaters this to be the case, see below). The calcite solubility constant is describe by;
CaCO3 (s) <--> Ca2+(aq) + CO32-(aq) (eq.3)

and;
Ksp-cal=(aCa2+)(aCO32-)=-8.480 at 25°C (SUPCRT92) (eq.4)

    Such that at simultaneous calcite-dolomite equilibrium;
2CaCO3 (s) + Mg2+(aq) <--> CaMg(CO3)(2)(s) + Ca2+(aq) (eq.5)

and the Ksp-dol can be defined as;
K(sp-dol) = (aCa2+ / aMg2+ ) . (1 / Ksp-cal) (eq.6)

    Therefore by evaluating aCa2+/aMg2+ using PHREEQC-Pitzer for each sample we can determine a value for K(sp-dol). Specifically as the Ksp is temperature dependent we fit aCa2+ / aMg2+ values and the corresponding at formation-depth temperature to a Meyer-Kelly type regressiom. We found the temperature depedency of Ksp-dol (more specifically pKsp−dol or log10Ksp−dol) to equal;
pKsp−dol = 1.47545×101 - 6.24959×10-2∙T(K) - 3.99350×103∙1/T(K) (eq.7)

    At 25°C pKsp°−dol is determined to be -17.27±0.35, which is close to prior estimates including the most recent experimentalally derived value reported by Bénézeth et al. (2018) (pKsp°−dol = -17.19±0.3).
    The interpretation that >90% of US groundwater samples are at calcite-dolomite equilibrium is made by the observation of the tight clustering of aCa2+/aMg2+ values around a constant increase with temperature (fig. 8). This relationship is consistent with prior observations of calcite-dolomite equilibrium (fig. 9).
2.1.5.1 Groundtruthing
To groundtruth the methods two test areas were selected. Test Area A encompasses an area of 9059km2 (bounding box W94°48'-W96°00'/N28°48'-N29°30') of SE Texas (fig. 10b). For the 21 PGWD samples from 9 different oil fields within Test Area A the geothermal model yields a mean surface temperature of 16°C (range 15-16°C) and a mean geothermal gradient of 33°C/km (range 29-35°C/km) (fig. 11a). This compares reasonably well with observations from both Morton and Land (1987) and Hyeong and Capuano (2001).
Test Area B encompasses an area of 13,224km2 along the Alabama-Mississippi border (fig. 10c). For the 204 PGWD samples, from 35 different oil fields, contained within Test Area B the geothermal model yields a mean surface temperature of 13°C (range 12-14°C) and a mean geothermal gradient of 27°C/km (range 22-34°C/km) (fig. 11b). This data compares well with the subsuface temperature observations of Kharaka et al. (1987).
With respect to geochemistry for both test areas we have compared the geochemical profiles of samples and the calculated aCa2+/aMg2+ values to literature data (Kharaka et al. 1987; Morton and Land, 1987; Hyeong and Capuano, 2001) finding consistent results.
